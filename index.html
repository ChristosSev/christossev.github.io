Christos Sevastopoulos
csevasto@uic.edu

I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Illinois Chicago, in the Richard and Loan Hill Department of Biomedical Engineering. I received my PhD in Computer Engineering from the University of Texas Arlington, advised by Filia Makedon. Before this, I completed my Master's in Robotics at the University of Bristol, UK. I earned my Bachelor's degree in Physics at the National & Kapodistrian University of Athens. Additionally, I have worked as a researcher at NCSR Demokritos under the supervision of Stasinos Konstantopoulos and as a Machine Learning Intern for GN GROUP.

My research lies at the crossroads of Deep Learning, Computer Vision, and Robotics, with the goal of developing machine learning systems that enhance decision-making. I’m particularly fascinated by transformer-based architectures, including models like BERT, and their synergy with multimodal data, which help agents better understand and navigate real-world environments. I’ve worked on improving scene understanding through techniques like image reconstruction, segmentation, and enhancement, using Generative AI methods such as diffusion models and GANs, along with object detection frameworks like YOLO and Faster R-CNN. Additionally, I explore how introducing noise—via adversarial attacks and diffusion processes—can enhance image quality and improve model robustness. A central theme in my work is the ongoing battle between human common sense and machine learning, aiming to bridge the gap between human-like reasoning and the structured learning processes of AI to improve decision-making in complex environments.

Publications

Few-shot Traversability Segmentation of Indoor Robotic Navigation with Contrastive Logits Align 1

Authors: Qiyuan An, Christos Sevastopoulos, Farnaz Farahanipad, Filia Makedon

This paper proposes a Few-Shot Learning (FSL) approach to meta-learn an existing pretrained segmentation model for an indoor traversability segmentation task. The goal is to meta-learn a segmentation model in a sense that it can adapt to a new unseen class given limited annotated examples.
